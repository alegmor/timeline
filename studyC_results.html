Study C · Bubble departure

Bubble growth and departure simulation

The aim of this work is to model pool boiling on copper surfaces with open rectangular microchannels in interThermalPhaseChangeFoam, and to validate the predicted bubble departure diameter for water and ethanol against the experimental data of Kaniowski et al.

What the animation shows
  • Nucleation. A vapour seed occupies the cavity and grows from the imposed wall superheat, expanding quasi-spherically into the liquid pool.
  • Growth with a pinned base. The bubble stays attached at the cavity lip while buoyancy builds; the interface rotates about the lip rather than sliding along the flat wall.
  • Necking and departure. A neck forms at the base and the bubble pinches off; the departure diameter is read from the volume of the detached region at pinch-off.
Contact-line geometry

The departure diameter follows from a balance between buoyancy on the vapour bubble and the surface-tension force along the three-phase contact line. The contact-line length \(L_c\) depends on how the surface is represented:

Switching from the two straight fin edges to the circular cavity lip raises \(L_c\) from \(2w\) to \(\pi w\), which increases the force-balance departure diameter by the factor \((\pi/2)^{1/3}\):

\( d_{b,\mathrm{axi}} = (\pi/2)^{1/3}\, d_{b,\mathrm{planar}} \)

Interpretation
The simulated departure diameter, \(1.34\ \mathrm{mm}\), lies between the fixed-angle axisymmetric estimate (\(1.00\ \mathrm{mm}\)) and the fully pinned upper bound (\(\approx 1.63\ \mathrm{mm}\)). Because \(d_b \propto (\sin\theta)^{1/3}\), a contact line that stays pinned at the sharp cavity lip while the apparent angle grows toward \(90^\circ\) permits release at a larger bubble than the static-angle balance predicts. The apparent angle inferred at release, \(\approx 34^\circ\), is well above the static \(13.4^\circ\), consistent with the interface rotating about the pinned lip rather than detaching at the static angle. This is the surface-tension / contact-line behaviour Study C is meant to expose in the continuum solver before it is coupled to the FLBM nucleation model.
